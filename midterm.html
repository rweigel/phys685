1 Long Cylinder Held at Potential

A long cylinder of radius b and centerline along the z–axis is held at potential Vo​sin2ϕ, where ϕ is the cylindrical coordinate angle. Assume that the potential at the origin is zero.

Find the potential inside of the cylinder.

Solution

As noted earlier, the statement “Assume that the potential at the origin is zero.” should not be there. In the following, I solve the problem assuming the potential at the origin was not given.

The general solution to ∇2ψ(s,ϕ)=0 is

ψ=Ao​+n=1∑∞​(An​cosnϕ+Bn​sinnϕ)sn+ao​lns+n=1∑∞​(an​cosnϕ+bn​sinnϕ)s−n

We can re–write sin2ϕ in terms of cosnϕ and sinnϕ using a trig identity, writing sinϕ in terms of complex exponentials, or by solving sin2ϕ=∑n=0∞​(an′​sinnϕ+bn′​cosnϕ) using Fourier’s trick. The result is sin2ϕ=(1−cos2ϕ)/2. At the boundary, we must have ψ(b,ϕ)=Vo​(1−cos2ϕ)/2.

We can solve this problem using superposition of A. a long cylinder held at Vo​/2 and B. a long cylinder held at Vo​cos2π/2.

For A., the potential will be constant inside of the cylinder. (There is no ϕ or z dependence, so charges will be uniformly distributed on the surface as a result of the cylinder being held at a fixed potential.) Inside of a uniformly charged cylinder, the electric field is zero (by Gauss’s law), so the potential will be constant. Another way of doing this is to simply note that ψA​=Vo​/2 satisfies the boundary condition and Laplace’s equation.

For B., the potential ψB​=(Vo​/2)(s2/b2)cos2ϕ satisfies the boundary condition and Laplace’s equation inside of the cylinder. We can drop the 1/sn and logs terms using the argument that the potential should be finite inside. The constant term is not needed to match the boundary condition, so it too can be dropped.

By superposition, then,

ψ=ψA​+ψB​=2Vo​​(1+b2s2​cosϕ)

The problem with setting the potential to zero at the origin is that this makes the problem much more complicated. Think of this boundary condition as being equivalent to holding the potential at zero on a small sphere centered on the origin. Charges will appear on the sphere such that near the origin, as the radius of the sphere approaches zero, the potential should approach that for a point charge, which is kq/r. For the boundary condition on the sphere, it is natural to use the spherical solution to Laplace’s equation. However, at the outer boundary, the cylindrical solution to Laplace’s equation is more natural.

A variation of this problem is to ask what happens if there is a grounded inner cylinder of radius a and a→0 and the outer cylinder is at Vo​. In this case, for s near zero, the field should be proportional to lns because the potential for s>a due to the charges on the conductor is the same as that for a line of charge on the z axis for s>a. But as s→0, lns→−∞, which makes it impossible to match the zero boundary condition. Said another way, the boundary value problem is to find Ao​ and Bo​ in

ψA​(s)=Ao​+Bo​lns

subject to

  1. ψA​(b)=Vo​

  2. ψB​(a)=0

From 1., ψA​(s)=Bo​lns/b. To satisfy 2., we need 0=Bo​ln0/b, or Bo​=0. With Bo​=0, the solution is ψA​=0, which does not satisify the boundary conditions.

2 Line of Charge

A line of charge with a charge density of λ is on the z-axis and extends from z=−1 to z=1.

For r>1, find the coefficients Al​ and Bl​ for the potential expressed in the form

ψ(r,θ)=l=0∑∞​(Al​rl+rl+1Bl​​)Pl​(cosθ)

First, we expect the leading order term to be 2kλL/r, which is the potential for a charge 2Lλ at the origin, where L is the length of the line (L=1 here).

Φ(z)=kλ∫z−1z+1​udu​=kλ[ln(z+1)−ln(z−1)]

We need to write this in a form so that we can use a power series expansion. This can be done by re–writing

Φ(z)=kλ[lnz(1+1/z)−lnz(1−1/z)]=kλ[ln(1+1/z)−ln(1−1/z)]

Using the expansion for δ<1

ln(1+δ)=δ+2δ2​+3δ3​+…

gives

Φ(z)=kλ(2z1​+32​z31​+52​z51​+…)

We could have also performed a power series expansion of 1/(z−z′) and integrated it:

Φ(z)=k∫−11​z−z′λdz′​=zkλ​∫−11​1−z′/z1dz′​

The integrand can be re–written using the binomial expansion

Φ(z)=zkλ​∫−11​(1−zz′​+z2z′2​+…)dz′

1–D Green Function for Cylindrical Geometry

A long non-conducting cylindrical shell of radius s′ and charge per length λ′ has its centerline along the z–axis.

A long conducting cylindrical shell of radius b<s′ and centerline along the z–axis is grounded.

A long conducting cylindrical shell of radius c>s′ and centerline along the z–axis is grounded.

The cross–section of this geometry is shown in the following figure.

1. Find the potential as a function of the cylindrical radial distance s.

2. Use Green’s second identity to find the potential when there is no non–conducting shell (or equivalently, λ′=0), the inner conducting shell is held at potential Vi​, and the outer conducting shell is held at potential Vo​.

Solution

1. The general solution to ∇2ψ(s)=0 is ψ(s)=A+Blns or, equivalently, ψ(s)=A′ln(s/B′). Laplace’s equation is satisfied in the inner (i) and outer (o) regions, so we seek solutions to

ψi​=Ai​lns/Bi​

ψo​=Ao​lns/Bo​

given the boundary conditions

  1. ψi​(b)=0

  2. ψo​(c)=0

  3. ψi​(s′)=ψo​(s′)

  4. [−∂ψo​/∂s+∂ψi​/∂s]s=s′​=σ′/ϵo​

We are given the charge per length (the length must be the length of the tube – if not, one could not solve). The total charge on the surface of the charged cylinder is λ′L. The total charge in terms of a σ is σ′2πs′L, so σ′=λ′/2πs′.

The result is

ψi​(s)=2πϵo​λ′​ln(b/c)ln(s′/c)​ln(s/b)

ψo​(s)=2πϵo​λ′​ln(b/c)ln(s′/b)​ln(s/c)

By inspection, these satify the first three boundary conditions and with a straightforward calculation can be shown to satisfy the fourth boundary condition.

2. The expected answer is Φ(s)=Vo​ln(s/b)/ln(c/b), which can be worked out by solving Φ(s)=A+Bln(s) subject to Φ(b)=0 and Φ(c)=Vo​.

Green’s second identity is

∫V​(Φ∇2ψ−ψ∇2Φ)d3x=∮S​(Φ∂n∂ψ​−ψ∂n∂Φ​)da

Let V be the empty volume between the conductors. The second integral on the RHS is zero because ψ=0 on the surface of V (spherical shells of radius b and c). The first surface integral on the RHS reduces to a single integral over the surface of a sphere of radius c, on which Φ=Vo​. The RHS is then

−Vo​∫02π​∫0L​dsdψo​​∣∣​s=c​dzdϕ

where n=−z was used. The charge denstiy in the ψ system is qδ(x−x′), where q=λ′L, so the first integral on the RHS is −qΦ(s′). The second term on the RHS is zero because the charge denstiy in the Φ system is zero. This leaves

−ϵo​λ′L​Φ(s′)=−∫02π​∫0L​dsdψo​​∣∣​s=c​cdzdϕ

Using

dsdψo​​∣∣​s=c​=2πϵo​λ′​ln(b/c)ln(s′/b)​c1​

and simplifying gives

Φ(s′)=Vo​ln(s′/b)/ln(c/b)

Given that s′ can vary between b and c, we can replace it with s and so

Φ(s)=Vo​ln(s/b)/ln(c/b)
